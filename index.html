Weekly Training

Gym work done immediately after climbing. Each session targets different patterns to avoid overlap with climbing's pulling demand.

DAY A
Climb + Lower Body, Calves & Core
~40 min after climbing · e.g. Mon

Barbell Back Squat
Full depth. Rest 2–3 min. Start moderate, increase weekly.

Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
Barbell or dumbbells. Feel the hamstring stretch at the bottom — no back rounding.

Dumbbell Bulgarian Split Squat
Rear foot elevated on bench. Exposes and corrects leg strength imbalances.

Calves
Standing Calf Raise (barbell on back)
Full range — deep stretch at bottom, full extension at top. Tempo: 2 up / 2 hold / 2 down. Hits gastrocnemius (outer visible calf shape).
4 × 10–12
Core (no rest between, 2 rounds)
Ab Wheel Rollout (or Barbell rollout)
Key for belly recomp. Slow eccentric. Stop before hips sag. Use barbell with plates as alternative to wheel.
2 × 8–12
Pallof Press (cable or resistance band)
Anti-rotation. Targets deep core and obliques directly. Attach band to rack or cable to side.
2 × 10/side
Hanging Leg Raise
On pull-up bar. Slow and controlled — no swinging. Tuck knees first if straight legs are too hard.
2 × 10–15
DAY B
Climb + Push & Shoulders
~35 min after climbing · e.g. Wed
▾
ChestShouldersTricepsCalvesBalances climbing's pulling
Main Work
Barbell Overhead Press (OHP)
Standing. The most important push movement for climbers. Builds shoulder stability and directly addresses the pull-dominated imbalance from bouldering.
4 × 6–8
Dumbbell Incline Bench Press
45° incline. Emphasises upper chest. Dumbbells allow natural wrist rotation — easier on the shoulders than a barbell.
3 × 8–10
Weighted Dips (dip bar + weight belt)
Lean slightly forward for chest emphasis. Control the descent slowly — 2–3 seconds down.
3 × 8–10
Cable / Band Lateral Raise
Single arm, cable or resistance band. Better than dumbbells — constant tension at the bottom where dumbbells go slack.
3 × 12–15
Overhead Tricep Extension (cable rope)
Long-head tricep focus. Use the rope attachment on a low cable or band anchored low. Pairs well with OHP day.
3 × 12–15
Calves
Seated Calf Raise (dumbbell on knee)
Sit on a bench, dumbbell on your knee, toes on a plate edge for range. Bent knee isolates the soleus — the deeper calf muscle that adds lower thickness. Heavy and slow.
4 × 12–15
DAY C
Climb + Posterior Chain & Arms
~40 min after climbing · e.g. Fri
▾
HamstringsWeighted Pull-upsBicepsRear deltsComplements climbing
Main Work
Barbell Deadlift
Conventional. The king of posterior chain. Keep back flat, brace hard, drive the floor away.
4 × 4–6
Weighted Pull-ups (weight belt)
Add 5–10kg. Full range — dead hang to chin above bar. These carry over directly to bouldering power.
4 × 5–6
Cable Face Pull (rope attachment)
Crucial for rotator cuff health as a climber. Pull to face level, externally rotate at the top. Never skip this.
3 × 15
Incline Dumbbell Curl
Long-head bicep emphasis. Let the arm hang back fully — the stretch at the bottom is where growth happens. Climbers have short-head bias from pulling.
3 × 10–12
Barbell Hip Thrust (or Dumbbell Glute Bridge)
Bench + barbell across hips. Or use a heavy dumbbell on your pelvis for a floor glute bridge. Completes the posterior chain that deadlifts miss at the top.
3 × 10–12

Key Principles

The science behind this plan. Understanding the why helps you stay consistent.

1
Belly fat is the last to go — be patient
You can't spot-reduce fat. But visceral/abdominal fat is metabolically active and responds strongly to resistance training + mild calorie deficit. Expect visible change in 8–12 weeks with consistency.
2
Gym after climbing, not before
Climbing requires fine motor skill and fresh fingers. Doing gym first fatigues your hands and degrades your climbing. Gym after preserves quality in both. The fatigue from climbing also pre-exhausts your pulling muscles, making weighted pull-ups more effective.
3
Push movements are your priority gap
Bouldering heavily loads pulling muscles (lats, biceps, forearms). Without dedicated pressing, you build a structural imbalance that causes shoulder injury over time. Day B (OHP + dips) directly addresses this.
4
Calves: full range of motion is everything
Partial reps do almost nothing. The deep stretch at the bottom is where growth happens. Standing barbell raises hit gastrocnemius (outer visible shape). Seated/dumbbell raises hit soleus (deeper, adds thickness). One exercise per day with full range and slow tempo is enough.
5
Protein before sleep = muscle repair
Casein protein (found in quark, cottage cheese, Greek yogurt) digests slowly over 6–7 hours. Eating it before bed means your muscles have a steady supply of amino acids during the night, preventing the catabolic state that typically occurs during overnight fasting.
6
The belly fat cortisol connection
Abdominal fat has 4× more cortisol receptors than fat elsewhere. Chronic stress, under-eating, and under-sleeping all spike cortisol — which specifically grows belly fat. Sleep and stress management are non-negotiable parts of this plan, not optional extras.
7
Track weight weekly, not daily
Daily weight fluctuates 1–2kg due to water, sodium, and glycogen. Weigh yourself once a week, same conditions (morning, after bathroom). You're aiming for –0.2 to –0.4kg/week. Faster than that and you risk losing muscle from your already-lean frame.
8
What to cut first for fastest belly results
In order of impact: (1) alcohol — causes visceral fat preferentially, (2) ultra-processed snacks, (3) liquid calories (juice, soft drinks), (4) large portions of refined carbs in the evening. You don't need to cut all at once — start with alcohol and processed snacks.
